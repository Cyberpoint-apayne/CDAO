Who Owns Cyber Data?

  • By: JAIC Public Affairs
  • 28  OCT 2020

At my previous DoD research organization, if I asked a cybersecurity service provider (CSSP) for operational data to execute test and evaluation for an AI capability developed on synthetic data, they may have laughed at me. Not because I told a joke, but because there was no clear answer to who owned the data, and they did not know who to ask. This was three years ago when I set out on my quest to determine who owns DoD’s cyber data and who is authorized to make decisions about sharing data on behalf of data owners. The answer is often as complicated as the quest to find the Holy Grail.

Depending on who you ask, the data owner often varies:

  • Services and combatant commands
    • Mission Owners
    • Combatant Commanders
    • US CYBERCOMMAND
  • DoD civilians
    • Sensor Owners
    • Organizational CIOs
    • Organizational that monitored the data, or configured the sensor to run
    • Original computer owner that generated the traffic.
  • An individual in the Human Research Protection Office (HRPO) and Institutional Review Board (IRB) may infer that whoever generates the data owns it - that's why study participants must sign a consent form.

Today, I am honored to lead the JAIC’s Joint Information Warfare mission initiative’s Field Study Team (FST). This is a new DoD concept, adopted from the private sector which has successfully used this approach for years. The FST takes a systematic approach with our DoD customers who desire access to AI capabilities that the JAIC is scaling across the Department, and more often than not, the question of data ownership is at the heart of this process.

The approach involves assessing a customer’s AI readiness by reviewing how their current system configurations and pain points align with their desired AI capabilities. We then match customers with AI capabilities that are tailored to the needs of their organization. As part of this process, the FST often takes on an advocacy role. This role is vital, and one that I enjoy, as the JAIC gets to know our customers and better understands their unique cyber data challenges. I am proud to amplify their voices to the highest levels of DoD policymaking.

Determining data stewardship is key to the important work occurring across the JAIC and DoD AI ecosystem. The recently released DoD Data Strategy says, “Data is a Strategic Asset – DoD data is a high-interest commodity and must be leveraged in a way that brings both immediate and lasting military advantage.” Specifically, when discussing data sets for AI training and algorithmic models, the strategy states that these data sets “will increasingly become the DoD’s most valuable digital assets and we must create a framework for managing them across the data lifecycle that provides protected visibility and responsible brokerage.” I am grateful for this strategy as a guidepost as we continue to determine who owns the operational data across the Department.

Back at the JAIC, the FST is working diligently with JAIC’s policy team, CDOs, and other key stakeholders to understand policies and identify who should work on these data ownership issues. This work is critical for our customers – and ourselves, as data access and sharing is truly where the rubber meets the road in order to scale AI. As the JAIC accelerates the delivery of AI across the Department, our talented data engineers and scientists can only truly succeed if they have access to relevant operational data and the ability to seamlessly share it across organizations.

Over the past couple months, the FST has moved the needle significantly. We recently began engaging with the DoD’s Chief Data Officer and are establishing a regular battle rhythm. We also look forward to working closely with the JAIC’s policy team, bringing all of the knowledge gained during our assessments to bear in order to inform them and hopefully change policies that will ultimately minimize existing data ownership barriers. We anticipate our efforts will make it easier for other DoD organizations to share their operational cyber data with the JAIC and each other, as we work together to transform the Department through AI.
